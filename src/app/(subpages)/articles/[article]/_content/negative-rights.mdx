## Two Kinds of Claims

### Leave Me Alone

Every claimed right belongs to one of two categories. **The first**: a claim that others refrain from acting against you. Don't assault me. Don't take my property. Don't compel my speech. These are [negative rights](/glossary/negative-rights). Defined entirely by the absence of interference.

**The second**: a claim that others provide you with something. House me. Treat my illness. Fund my education. These are [positive rights](/glossary/positive-rights). Defined by an obligation someone else must fulfill.

The difference is not cosmetic. A negative right imposes no burden on any specific person. It asks only that everyone do nothing. A positive right necessarily assigns a duty to a third party. Someone must build the house, staff the clinic, pay the teacher. That assignment either happens voluntarily, through contract or gift, or it happens by force.

### The Asymmetry

This is where the two categories diverge completely. Negative rights are _universally stackable_: every person on earth can simultaneously hold the right not to be assaulted without that arrangement generating any contradiction. Your right to be left alone costs me nothing except restraint.

Positive rights cannot stack the same way. If every person holds a right to housing, then every person also holds an obligation to provide it, or to fund its provision. The moment the obligation is assigned without consent, it overrides someone's negative right to their own labour and property. **One person's positive right becomes another person's coerced duty.**

### Declared, Not Discovered

Negative rights can be _derived_ — they follow logically from the fact of self-ownership. Positive rights are _declared_. The 1948 UN Declaration did exactly this, adding rights to rest, housing, and a standard of living adequate for health.

## Ownership and NAP

### What Ownership Means

Before asking what rights you have, it is worth asking what it means to _own_ something at all. Ownership is not a feeling or a social convention. It is a claim about decision-making authority: the owner is the person whose judgment about a resource is final. Who may use it, exchange it, or exclude others from it.

The most basic and important ownership claim is over yourself. You are the one inside your body. You bear the consequences of what it does and what is done to it. No one experiences your hunger, your pain, or your choices from the inside.

From [Self-ownership](/glossary/self-ownership), property follows by a consistent chain. If you own yourself, you own your labour. If you own your labour, you own what you produce with it or what you voluntarily acquire by exchanging it. **Ownership of external things is an extension of self-ownership**, not a separate grant, not a social permission, but the same logic applied outward.

### The NAP

Negative rights are what self-ownership looks like from the outside: a boundary around each person that no one may cross without consent. The Non-Aggression Principle simply names that boundary as a rule. If you own yourself and your legitimately acquired property, then initiating force against you is always a violation of that ownership, regardless of who initiates it or why.

The [Non-Aggression Principle](/glossary/non-aggression-principle) is not a complete ethics. It does not say lying is acceptable, or that breaking a promise carries no moral weight. It says those wrongs belong to a different category. One that may be condemned, shamed, and socially sanctioned, but that does not justify a physical response from a third party. The NAP draws a line between wrongs that may be physically resisted and wrongs that may not. That line matters precisely because it is narrow and consistent.

The NAP does not change its answer based on who is doing the coercing or how sympathetic the stated goal is. A mugger who needs rent and a state collecting taxes are performing structurally identical acts. The transfer is non-consensual and backed by a threat of force. Whether one is _legitimate_ and the other is not is a separate question. The NAP simply insists the structure be named honestly.

## Rights and Order

### Where This Leads

Take negative rights and the NAP seriously, and a natural question follows: **what institutions are consistent with them?**

This tension splits libertarian thought into two camps that share the same foundation but reach opposite conclusions. [Minarchists](/glossary/minarchism) argue that a minimal state, limited strictly to protecting negative rights and adjudicating disputes, can be justified, because some form of monopoly on force is necessary to prevent worse violations. [Anarcho-capitalists](/glossary/anarcho-capitalism) reject the exception: if initiating force is wrong, it does not become right when the institution doing it calls itself a state. They argue that protection and dispute resolution can be provided by competing voluntary institutions, none holding a territorial monopoly, all accountable to exit.

**The deeper point is that negative rights, if taken consistently, push toward [polycentric law](/glossary/polycentric-law)**, multiple competing legal frameworks that individuals choose between, rather than a single system imposed by geography and birth.

## What Remains Open

### An Open Question

The argument for negative rights is internally consistent. That consistency does not make it complete.

If negative rights require enforcement, and enforcement requires an institution with coercive power, does that institution itself violate the rights it claims to protect? Can it justify compelling payment from those who did not contract with it? These are not rhetorical questions designed to smuggle statism back in. They are difficulties that only appear once you take the argument seriously enough to follow it to its end.

### Not Only Principled

The case for NAP is not purely deontological. Secure property rights reduce the cost of planning and investment. Voluntary exchange generates information that coerced redistribution destroys. Coercion is economically destructive because it violates ownership, it severs the link between effort and reward, between a person's judgment and the consequences of that judgment.

**The NAP is not just the most principled foundation for social order — it is likely the most productive one.**
