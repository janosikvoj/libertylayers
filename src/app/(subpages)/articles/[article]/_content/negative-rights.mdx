## Two Kinds of Claims

### Leave Me Alone

Every claimed right belongs to one of two categories. The first: a claim that others refrain from acting against you. Don't assault me. Don't take my property. Don't compel my speech. These are [negative rights](/glossary/negative-rights) — defined entirely by the absence of interference.

The second: a claim that others provide you with something. House me. Treat my illness. Fund my education. These are [positive rights](/glossary/positive-rights) — defined by an obligation someone else must fulfill.

The difference is not cosmetic. A negative right imposes no burden on any specific person. It asks only that everyone do nothing. A positive right necessarily assigns a duty to a third party — someone must build the house, staff the clinic, pay the teacher. That assignment either happens voluntarily, through contract or gift, or it happens by force.

### The Asymmetry

Negative rights are universally stackable. Every person on earth can simultaneously hold the right not to be assaulted without that arrangement generating any contradiction. Your right to be left alone costs me nothing except restraint.

Positive rights cannot stack the same way. If every person holds a right to housing, then every person also holds an obligation to provide it — or to fund its provision. The moment the obligation is assigned without consent, it overrides someone's negative right to their own labour and property. **One person's positive right becomes another person's coerced duty.**

Positive rights are not derived — they are declared. The 1948 UN Declaration announced a right to rest, to paid holidays, to a standard of living adequate for health.

## Ownership and NAP

### What Ownership Means

Before asking what rights you have, it is worth asking what it means to _own_ something at all. Ownership is not a feeling or a social convention. It is a claim about decision-making authority: the owner is the person whose judgment about a resource is final — who may use it, exchange it, or exclude others from it.

The most basic ownership claim is over yourself. You are the one inside your body. You bear the consequences of what it does and what is done to it. No one experiences your hunger, your pain, or your choices from the inside. [Self-ownership](/glossary/self-ownership) is therefore not a political position — it is a description of how persons relate to themselves prior to any social arrangement.

From self-ownership, property follows by a consistent chain. If you own yourself, you own your labour. If you own your labour, you own what you produce with it or what you voluntarily acquire by exchanging it. **Ownership of external things is an extension of self-ownership** — not a separate grant, not a social permission, but the same logic applied outward.

### The NAP

The [Non-Aggression Principle](/glossary/non-aggression-principle) follows directly. If you own yourself and your legitimately acquired property, then initiating force against you — without your consent and without prior aggression on your part — is always a violation. This is not a consequence of utility or social welfare. It is a consequence of what ownership means.

_Coercion is wrong because of what it does to the person coerced, not because of what it fails to achieve._

The NAP is a principle about a specific category of action: the initiation of physical force or the credible threat of it. It is not a complete ethics. It does not say lying is acceptable, or that breaking a promise carries no moral weight. It says those wrongs belong to a different category — one that may be condemned, shamed, and socially sanctioned, but that does not justify a physical response from a third party. **The NAP draws a line between wrongs that may be physically resisted and wrongs that may not.** That line matters precisely because it is narrow and consistent.

The NAP does not change its answer based on who is doing the coercing or how sympathetic the stated goal is. A mugger who needs rent and a state collecting taxes are performing structurally identical acts — the transfer is non-consensual and backed by a threat of force. Whether one is _legitimate_ and the other is not is a separate question. The NAP simply insists the structure be named honestly.

## Rights and Order

### Where This Leads

Take negative rights and the NAP seriously, and a natural question follows: what institutions are consistent with them?

Most libertarian thinkers converge on the same answer from different directions. A minimal state — protecting only negative rights, funded voluntarily or through narrowly consented taxation — is the position of [minarchists](/glossary/minarchism). A fully voluntary legal order, where protection and dispute resolution are provided by competing private institutions rather than a territorial monopoly, is the position of [anarcho-capitalists](/glossary/anarcho-capitalism). Both positions share the same foundation. They differ only on whether a rights-respecting monopoly on force is coherent or contradictory.

**The deeper point is that negative rights, if taken consistently, push toward [polycentric law](/glossary/polycentric-law)** — multiple competing legal frameworks that individuals choose between, rather than a single system imposed by geography and birth. Whether or not that destination is desirable, it is where the logic leads.

## What Remains Open

### An Open Question

The argument for negative rights is internally consistent. That consistency does not make it complete.

If negative rights require enforcement, and enforcement requires an institution with coercive power, does that institution itself violate the rights it claims to protect? Can it justify compelling payment from those who did not contract with it? If it cannot, how does it fund itself without becoming what it opposes?

These are not rhetorical questions designed to smuggle statism back in. They are difficulties that only appear once you take the negative-rights argument seriously enough to follow it to its end.

**The strongest case for negative rights is not that it solves every problem.** It is that it refuses to solve problems by pretending coercion doesn't count as a cost — and that this refusal, uncomfortable as it is, is the only starting point from which honest answers become possible.

When someone demands "someone must provide this," the next question is always: which someone, chosen how, by whom, and what happens to them if they refuse?
