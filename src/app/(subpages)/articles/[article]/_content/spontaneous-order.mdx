## The Hobbesian Assumption

### Order vs. Ruler

In 1651, Thomas Hobbes described life outside political authority as "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." Without a sovereign, he argued, there could be no industry, no culture, no cooperation — only a war of all against all. This premise has shaped political philosophy ever since. It is, in most forms it takes, a category error.

The error is conflating the absence of a _ruler_ with the absence of _order_. These are not the same thing. A forest has no ruler. A language has no ministry. The common law of England accumulated over centuries without a legislature. The internet's foundational protocols were written by engineers who held no state authority over anyone. In none of these cases does the absence of central design produce chaos. In most of them, it produces something more adaptive and more complex than any designer could have produced intentionally.

### The Intuition to Dismantle

The Hobbesian intuition is powerful because it feels obvious. We are used to artifacts: chairs, cities, legal codes, organisations. Artifacts are designed. We reverse-engineer that logic onto everything we encounter — if something works, someone must have planned it. **But most of what works was not planned.** The assumption of design where none exists is not a neutral observation. It is a bias — and a politically consequential one.

## Order Without Design

### The Exhibits

Consider what the following have in common: spoken language, the common law, money, the internet's TCP/IP stack, scientific consensus, trade routes, etiquette, double-entry bookkeeping, and the price system. All are extraordinarily complex. All coordinate the behaviour of millions or billions of people. None were centrally designed.

Each of these systems emerged from the same basic mechanism: **local interactions, repeated over time, producing stable patterns that no participant intended and no observer could have predicted in advance.** The pattern is not accidental — it is the consistent output of free interaction among agents pursuing their own ends.

### Language

No committee invented English grammar. No sovereign decreed that the past tense would be formed the way it is, or that "cool" would come to mean what it now means. Grammar crystallised from millions of conversations. Words drifted in meaning through use. Dialects diverged. Pidgins stabilised into creoles. The system is self-correcting: expressions that communicate well spread; those that don't, fade.

_What is remarkable is not that language has structure, but that its structure is more precise, more nuanced, and more flexible than any designed alternative._ The Académie Française has spent four centuries trying to prescribe correct French. The language has ignored most of it.

### Common Law

English common law was not enacted. It accumulated. Judges resolved disputes by appeal to precedent — prior decisions that had themselves resolved prior disputes. Each ruling added a small increment of doctrine. Over centuries, an extraordinarily detailed body of property law, contract law, and tort law emerged without any legislature having sat down to design it.

**The common law did not require a planner because it had something better: a feedback mechanism.** Bad precedents produced perverse outcomes, which produced pressure to distinguish or overturn them. Good precedents held because they resolved disputes cleanly. The system learned — not because anyone instructed it to, but because error was costly and survival favoured correction.

### The Internet

The internet's core protocols — TCP/IP, HTTP, SMTP — were not designed by a government. They were written by engineers, adopted voluntarily because they solved coordination problems, and refined through use. No authority compelled anyone to adopt them. They spread because they worked.

The result is the largest and most complex communication infrastructure in human history, routing hundreds of billions of messages daily across millions of independent networks, with no central node and no central controller. **It is more resilient, more adaptive, and more capable than any designed alternative** — precisely because its architecture assumes no single point of control.

## The State as Machine

### Wrong Model

The state, in its modern form, treats society as a mechanism. There are inputs — tax revenues, legislative mandates, regulatory powers. There are outputs — intended social outcomes. There are levers: adjust the lever, change the output. This model is not malicious. It is simply the wrong ontology applied to the wrong class of system.

Mechanisms have fixed structure. You can stand outside a clock and adjust its gears. Complex adaptive systems have no outside. The planner who attempts to optimise the economy is part of the economy. The regulator who redesigns incentives is subject to incentives. The legislator who rewrites norms is embedded in norms. **There is no Archimedean point from which the system can be observed whole and adjusted without distortion.**

### Design vs. Emergence

The contrast is not between order and chaos. It is between two kinds of order: designed order, which is as complex as its designer, and emergent order, which can vastly exceed the complexity of any individual within it.

By the time the plan is implemented, the conditions it was designed for have already changed.

## Hayek's Insight

### Distributed Knowledge

Friedrich Hayek's contribution was to name what makes complex systems irreducible to design. The knowledge required to run a society — or even a single market — is not held in any mind or any database. It is dispersed across every participant: the farmer who knows her soil, the tradesman who knows his customers, the consumer who knows her own preferences and constraints.

This knowledge is not merely _uncompiled_. Much of it is **tacit** — it exists only in practice, in habit, in judgment developed through experience. It cannot be reported upward because it cannot be articulated downward. It is the kind of knowledge that disappears the moment you try to formalise it.

_Designing a complex system requires compressing what cannot be compressed._ The attempt does not fail for lack of effort. It fails structurally — because the compression destroys the information content that made the system work.

### Complexity and Humility

This is not an argument for passivity. It is an argument about where the burden of proof lies. Spontaneous orders — language, law, money, science — emerged over centuries of trial, error, and selection. They encode an enormous amount of distributed knowledge in their structure, accumulated through a process no designer could replicate or accelerate without corrupting it.

**An intervention that overrides a spontaneous order is not a neutral act.** It is a claim that the intervener possesses knowledge superior to the accumulated distributed knowledge encoded in the system. That claim requires justification — not rhetorical justification, but epistemic justification. Can the intervener actually process the information the system was processing? If not, what is lost in the override?

## What Remains Open

### An Open Question

If the most successful coordination systems in history were never designed, what does that imply about the burden of proof?

The standard political argument runs: here is a problem; here is an intervention; the intervention addresses the problem; therefore the intervention is justified. But this argument omits a comparison class. Does the intervention improve on what spontaneous processes would have produced? Or does it merely _intend_ to — and count intention as sufficient?

Hayek's challenge is not that intervention always fails. It is that the default assumption — that design is an improvement on emergence — has the epistemics backwards. **The burden should sit with the designer, not with the order being overridden.**

What would it look like to take that burden seriously? What standard of evidence would a proposed intervention need to meet to justify disrupting a system that has been encoding distributed knowledge for decades or centuries? That question does not have a tidy answer. But it is the right question to be asking — and most political discourse has not yet begun to ask it.
