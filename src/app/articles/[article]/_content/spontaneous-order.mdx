When people talk about human rights, the conversation often becomes a
chaotic wish list. We hear about the "right" to healthcare,
the "right" to a living wage, or the "right" to
high-speed internet. While these might be desirable societal goals,
framing them as rights creates a fundamental, unresolvable ethical
contradiction. To build a consistent ethical framework for a free
society, we must understand the critical difference between wanting
something and having a right to it. This requires defining freedom not
as an entitlement to the labor of others, but purely as the absence of
coercion.
